Remember vs Remind — та як ставити запитання

Відмінності у вживанні; форми питань у різних часах

remember vs remind

When we remember something, we have a memory of this event or person.

For example:
• I remember our meeting. (I have this memory, I know when and where it happened.)
• Do you remember Mark? (You know who this person is, you met before.)

When someone/something reminds us of or about, they want us to have this memory again or bring it back to us.

For example:
• The smell of strawberries reminds me of summer. (Every time I smell strawberries, it makes me think about summer.)
• I reminded Mark about the birthday party. (I told him about it, so he remembers about it now.)

Pay attention!
• This song reminds me of our first dance. (It happens when some smell, music, smile brings back the memory and makes you think of something else.)
• Remind me about our meeting, please. (use it when you want someone to remember some task, event or obligation)

In subject questions the question word IS the subject, so we can’t put an auxiliary / modal verb before the subject. That’s why we use the word order of a regular sentence.

Pay attention:
Who / What is always third person singular, even if we know that the answer is plural.

For example:
Who knows the answer? - We all know the answer.
What has happened? - The cats have had a fight.
What brings you here? - we use it to ask why the person is at some specific place. It’s a great way to start a conversation.

For example:
A: Hey, Tony! Long time no see! What brings you here?
B: Oh, hey! I’m in town for the conference.
A: Do you want to grab a beer or something?
B: I’d love to. It’ll be great to catch up. How about tonight?
A: Sounds great.

Use it right!
When telling a joke or story, we use present simple tense to show the event in a dynamic manner.
