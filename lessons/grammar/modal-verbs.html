Modal verbs — can, must, have to, should

Значення, форма, використання, приклади

MODAL EXAMPLE
It is allowed / permitted

Ability = be able to 		Can Stacy can visit night clubs because she is 18.
(it’s permitted)
You can learn to drive when you're seventeen in the UK.
I can hold my breath for 2 minutes.
(Ability)
It isn’t allowed / permitted Can’t
Mustn’t 		You can’t smoke in public places.
You can't come in here with your shoes on.
You mustn't go into the garden.
(Because it’s forbidden)
It’s a good / bad idea Should / Shouldn’t You should eat a lot of fruit and vegetables.
(They are very useful and it’s a good idea)
You shouldn’t go to bed late before an important exam.
(That’s a bad idea because you will be sleepy)
It is necessary Have to / Has to
Must 		Marta is a police officer. She has to wear uniform.
(That’s a rule and she has no choice)
You must exercise at least 3 times per week to lose some weight.
(It is a strong recommendation)
I have to get up early tomorrow because my train leaves at 7 a.m.
It is not necessary Don’t have to /
Doesn’t have to 		Helen is a student and she doesn’t have to wear uniform.
(There is no rule about wearing uniform in her university. But she can wear it if she wants)
Do you have to do military service? – No, I don’t.
(I don't have to do military service, because I have health problems)
Use it right!
Must vs Have to in affirmative sentences:
Use 'must' when the speaker decides that something is necessary.
e.g.: You must exercise at least 3 times per week (the speakers thinks it is necessary)
Use 'have to' when somebody / something else decides it e.g. a rule / regulation / instruction.
e.g.: She has to wear a uniform (according to the dress code rules)
