Past Simple vs Present Perfect
PAST SIMPLE PRESENT PERFECT
  1. An action which happened at a stated time in the past.
    e.g.: We met my school friend yesterday.
    Eugene wasn’t at work yesterday.
    Did Liza live in Ukraine in 2010?
  2. An action which started and finished in the past.
    e.g.: I had a cat when I lived with my parents.
    (I don’t have a cat anymore)
  1. An action which happened at an unstated time in the past.
    e.g.: We have jumped with a parachute. (We don’t know when it happened, the result is more interesting)
    He hasn’t bought a new car yet.
    Have you ever been to India?
  2. An action which started in the past and is still continuing in the present.
    e.g.: We have known Tony since childhood. (We still know each other)

Spelling rules
Below are three basic rules that demonstrate how to add the ed ending to verbs.
+ ed to most words. If the word ends in e, + d rate + ed = rated
work + ed = worked
If words end in y after a consonant, change y to i and + ed study + ed = studied
carry + ed = carried
If the word is short and ends in consonant + vowel + consonant, double the final consonant and + ed stop + ed=stopped
wrap + ed = wrapped
Never double w,x,y or z fix + ed = fixed
stay + ed = stayed

Yet / Just / Already
Already means something has happened before now (probably sooner than expected). It is usually used in positive sentences, directly before the main verb or at the end of the sentence. e.g.: You've already told me that.
He's taken his driving test six times already.
Yet in negative sentences means something has not happened, but will probably happen at some point in the future. In questions, we use it to find out if something has happened before now. It usually comes at the end of the negative sentence / question. e.g.: I haven't bought the tickets yet. Have you seen Dave yet?
Just means something has happened a short time ago. It is usually used in positive sentences, directly before the main verb. e.g.: I've just seen a really great film. Have you just arrived?
Ever and Never
Have you ever broken your leg?
I've never tried Japanese food.=I haven’t tried Japanese food.

We often use ever and never with the Present Perfect Simple when we are talking about experience.
Ever (in questions) means at any point up until now.

Never means at no point in the past up until now. With never, we use the verb in the affirmative
(NOT in the negative).
She hasn't never been to Italy.
